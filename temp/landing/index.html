The City CO2 Project

At the University of British Columbia's Urban Climate Group, we are currently mobilizing a fleet of CO2 sensors to better map and understand the changing landscape of CO2 emissions in the city of Vancouver, BC, Canada. With these measurements, we can begin to quanitfy and visualize the local scale variability of CO2 concentrations at a scale and resolution like never before.

Along with building a fleet of mobile sensors, we develop a web-based CO2 mapping application to visually explore the data measured throughout the city. By giving the public, practicioners, policy makers, and designers the tools to visually explore the direct impacts of people and the city on the environment, we hope to faciliate new ways of approaching and developing solutions for a low-carbon future.

Why is this important? Cities and the cumulative processes of urbanization are estimated to contribute nearly 70% of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions worldwide. Cities are undeniably a major contributor to increasing GHG concentrations in the earth's atmosphere, enhancing greenhouse gas forcing and increasing the uncertainty our future climate.

Current tools monitoring our GHG emissions can not give us feedback at the spatial scales we can relate to and plan around. Traditional methods operate at coarse spatial resolutions (e.g. measured from space over thousands of kilometers) or at sparsely located fixed points. How can we as communities and individuals begin to make more conscious decisions about reducing our emissions if we dont have the tools to take ownership of our emissions?

Climate change is indeed a global phenomenon, but through local-scale policy interventions, urban design, community participation, and behavioral change, cities hold a great potential to reduce their carbond footprint and can lead the way to a more sustainable future.

Overview: From the lab to the streets

  Building the Tools

Inspired by opensourced technologies, we are using Arduino to power our CO2 sensor system. The versatility of the Arduino has allowed us to plug-and-play the Adafruit GPS shield (latitude, longitude, vehicle speed, and elevation) with a LiCor CO2 Infrared Gas Analyzer, as well as a digital thermometer. The sensor is approximately 35.8cm x 27.8cm x 11.8cm and weighs approx. 1.5kgs.

  Mobilizing the Fleet

Designed to be portable by cars and bikes, we aim to measure greenhouse gas concentrations at high spatial and temporal resolution across all parts of the city. The idea is to "piggyback" on existing infrastructure so that our measurements can be done intensively and extensively and to minimize the carbon footprint of our research efforts.

With the addition of these sensors to vehicles, in particular mobility service providers like Car2Go, we can take "mobility-as-a-service" to the next level by providing new information about the city's environment to it's residents.

  Data Analysis

With the measured data, we will be analyzing various aspects of the elevated concentrations of CO2 in cities, often referred to as the "urban CO2 dome". We want to explore these key questions:

  • Where is the variability in CO2 concentrations the highest and what does this tell us about the emissions sources near by?
  • How does the urban CO2 dome change over time and across different land use types? How does the landscape and season affect the CO2 dome?
  • What are the issues surrounding the sampling density of a mobile sensor fleet? Is it a viable option for monitoring such pollutants?

  Visualization

Along with the tools to measure and monitor CO2, we also build a set of interactive web based mapping and visualization tools to examine spatial and temporal dimensions of the CO2 data. We invite people to filter and aggregate by date and time, CO2 range, neighborhood and mode of transport and retrieve data for further analysis using our application programming interface (API). The dataviewer is targeted to engage communities, citizen scientists, planners and policy makers to better identify emissions patterns (if any), help raise questions about why these patterns emerge, and in-turn develop local and regional solutions for addressing these questions.
