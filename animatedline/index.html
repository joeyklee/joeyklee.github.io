Visualizing Your City CO2
A Mobile Sensing Approach: Currently, the tools don't exist to monitor and map greenhouse gases (GHGs) in cities, particularly for CO2. At UBC, we're developing sensors that can be attached to vehicles and bikes so that we can map GHGs at policy relevant scales, from your community to the metropolitan region. By capturing these microscale concentrations we might be able to develop strategies to reduce our impact on the environment. Check out this animated visualization of CO2 measurements taken from one of our mobile sensors.

Background

The global average CO2 mixing ratio is 397.8 ppm. This means that globally, this is the baseline of CO2 concentrations in the atmosphere. Any measured value above this level is CO2 that is put into the atmosphere through fossil fuel combustion from heating, ventilation, and cooling systems, transportation, industrial processes, human respiration, and to a lesser degree plant respiration.



Modeled global CO2 emissions. Source: NASA

However, the current tools monitoring our GHG emissions can not give us feedback at the spatial scales we can relate to and plan around. Traditional methods operate at coarse spatial resolutions (e.g. measured from space over thousands of kilometers) or at sparsely located fixed points.How can we as communities and individuals begin to make more conscious decisions about reducing our emissions if we dont have the tools to take ownership of our emissions?


A Mobile CO2 Monitoring Platform

Given improvements in technology and the growth of the opensource community, new opportunities exist to help benchmarch our emissions in our communities. Using a mobile approach to sensing and monitoring emissions, we can use cars and bikes to measure greenhouse gas concentrations at a local level/neighborhood level.


Basic Schematic diagram of compact, mobile CO2 sensor platform


Reducing GHG emissions will be critical for mitigating global climate uncertainty. When looking at the measured emissions along the various routes, notice where concentrations might be high or low. Do you notice any trends or patterns?
